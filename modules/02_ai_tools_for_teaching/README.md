# Module 02 — AI Tools for Teaching

**Duration:** ~4 hours  
**Prerequisites:** [Module 01 — Introduction to AI in Didactics and Research](../01_introduction/)

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

- Use AI to design complete lesson plans and curricula.
- Generate and adapt educational content for different learner levels.
- Create diverse assessment items (quizzes, rubrics, case studies) with AI assistance.
- Apply AI tools to provide personalised, scalable feedback on student work.
- Identify when AI assistance adds value in teaching and when it does not.

---

## 1. Lesson Planning and Curriculum Design

AI tools excel at helping educators structure their thinking and generate initial drafts — saving hours of planning time.

### What AI can do

- Generate lesson outlines for any subject and level.
- Suggest learning objectives aligned to Bloom's Taxonomy.
- Propose differentiated activities for diverse learners.
- Create pacing guides and multi-week curriculum structures.
- Translate a learning outcome into a sequence of activities.

### Prompt patterns for lesson planning

**Basic lesson outline:**
```
Create a 90-minute lesson plan for a university course on [topic].
Learner level: [undergraduate / master's / PhD].
Learning objective: Students will be able to [specific skill or knowledge].
Include: learning objectives, warm-up activity, main content, active learning exercise, and closing summary.
```

**Bloom's Taxonomy alignment:**
```
I am teaching [topic] to [audience].
Generate one learning objective for each level of Bloom's Taxonomy
(remember, understand, apply, analyse, evaluate, create).
```

**Differentiated instruction:**
```
I have a mixed-ability group of [number] students studying [topic].
Some have strong background in [area]; others are beginners.
Suggest three variations of the same activity at different difficulty levels.
```

### Tips

- Always review AI-generated plans critically — they may omit discipline-specific nuance.
- Use the AI output as a first draft, not a final product.
- Ask the AI to justify its design choices: `Why did you structure the lesson this way?`

## 2. Generating and Adapting Educational Content

### Content generation use cases

| Task | Example prompt |
|------|---------------|
| Explain a concept | `Explain [concept] using an analogy suitable for a student with no background in [field].` |
| Simplify text | `Rewrite this paragraph at a B2 English level, keeping all key ideas.` |
| Create examples | `Give me 5 real-world examples of [concept] relevant to [discipline].` |
| Generate case studies | `Write a realistic case study about [topic] for use in a seminar discussion. Include discussion questions.` |
| Create glossaries | `Define these 10 terms in plain language suitable for first-year students: [list].` |
| Translate content | `Translate this handout from English to Polish, maintaining academic register.` |

### Adapting existing materials

You can paste your own text into an AI tool and ask it to:
- Shorten or expand it.
- Adjust the reading level.
- Reformat it as bullet points, tables, or a Q&A.
- Add examples or analogies.
- Create a slide deck outline from it.

> **Important:** Do not paste confidential student data or unpublished research into commercial AI tools without checking your institution's data privacy policy.

## 3. AI-Assisted Assessment Design

### Generating assessment items

AI can quickly generate diverse question types:

**Multiple-choice questions:**
```
Write 10 multiple-choice questions on [topic] suitable for a [level] exam.
Include one correct answer and three plausible distractors for each question.
Indicate the correct answer and the reasoning behind each distractor.
```

**Open-ended questions:**
```
Generate 5 essay questions on [topic] that require students to apply,
analyse, or evaluate — not just recall information.
```

**Problem sets:**
```
Create 6 problem-solving exercises on [topic] at increasing difficulty levels,
from straightforward application to complex multi-step problems.
```

**Rubrics:**
```
Create a grading rubric for an essay assignment on [topic].
The essay is 1500 words. Criteria should include: argument quality,
evidence use, structure, and academic writing style.
Use a 4-level scale (Excellent / Good / Developing / Beginning).
```

### Formative assessment ideas

AI can help you design quick formative checks:
- Exit tickets and reflection prompts
- Peer review guidance
- Self-assessment checklists
- "Muddiest point" surveys with follow-up explanations

## 4. Personalised Learning and Student Support

### AI as a teaching assistant

Many institutions are deploying AI chatbots (powered by LLMs) to answer student questions 24/7. You can also guide your students to use general AI tools responsibly for:

- Getting additional explanations of difficult concepts.
- Checking their understanding through Q&A.
- Generating practice problems and checking their own solutions.
- Receiving feedback on draft essays or reports.

### Personalised explanations at scale

A practical technique: ask students to use AI to explain a concept to them in a different way if they don't understand your explanation:

```
I am studying [concept] and I don't understand it.
Can you explain it to me as if I were [analogy or background]?
Then give me a simple example I can work through myself.
```

### Supporting diverse learners

- **Language support:** AI can translate or simplify course materials for non-native speakers.
- **Accessibility:** AI can describe images, generate audio scripts, or simplify dense academic text.
- **Neurodiversity:** AI can restructure information into lists, tables, or visual formats.

## 5. Providing Feedback on Student Work

AI can assist with feedback at scale, but human judgment remains essential.

### AI-assisted feedback workflow

1. Define your feedback criteria clearly (e.g., a rubric).
2. Paste the student text (anonymised) into the AI tool.
3. Ask for feedback aligned to your criteria:

```
Here is a student essay on [topic]. Please provide constructive feedback
on the following criteria: [list criteria]. Be specific, pointing to
exact passages. Suggest concrete improvements. Do not rewrite the essay.
```

4. Review the AI feedback before sharing it with the student.
5. Add your own comments and professional judgment.

### Limitations

- AI cannot assess lab work, oral presentations, or non-text submissions without additional tools.
- AI feedback may miss discipline-specific expectations.
- Always verify that AI feedback is fair, accurate, and appropriate before sending.

## 6. AI for Student Assessment of AI (Meta-skills)

Embedding AI literacy into your curriculum helps students become critical AI users:

### Discussion activities

- Give students an AI-generated text and ask them to identify errors, biases, or missing nuance.
- Ask students to improve an AI-generated essay and justify their changes.
- Have students debate the ethics of AI use in a specific scenario in your field.

### Assignment designs that work alongside AI

- **Process portfolios:** Students document their thinking process, not just the final product.
- **Oral defenses:** Students explain and justify their submitted work in person.
- **Iterative reflection:** Students submit AI interactions alongside their work, with annotations.
- **Novel application tasks:** Ask students to apply concepts to new cases or personal experiences that AI cannot easily generalise to.

## 7. Hands-On Exercise: Design a Lesson with AI

**Task:** Use an AI tool to design a complete lesson plan for one session in a course you teach or plan to teach.

Steps:
1. Write a clear learning objective for your lesson.
2. Use AI to generate a lesson outline.
3. Use AI to generate at least one activity or resource (e.g., a case study, question set, or reading summary).
4. Use AI to draft a rubric or assessment for the lesson.
5. Review all AI outputs critically and revise as needed.
6. Save your final lesson plan for submission as [Assignment 02](../../assignments/assignment_02_lesson_plan.md).

## Reflection Questions

1. Which teaching tasks do you spend the most time on? Could AI reduce that time meaningfully?
2. Where does AI assistance risk undermining the quality or authenticity of teaching?
3. How would you explain appropriate AI use to your students?
4. What institutional support would you need to integrate AI into your teaching practice?

---

## Key Takeaways

- AI can dramatically accelerate routine teaching tasks: planning, content creation, assessment design.
- The educator's role shifts toward quality control, critical review, and professional judgment.
- Effective AI-assisted teaching requires clear criteria, careful prompting, and human oversight.
- AI can also become a subject of teaching — helping students develop their own AI literacy.
