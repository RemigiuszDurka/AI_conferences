## 1. What Is Artificial Intelligence?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to computer systems that can perform tasks which typically require human intelligence — such as understanding language, recognising images, solving problems, and generating creative content.

Modern AI is not a single technology. It includes:

- **Machine Learning (ML):** Systems that learn patterns from data rather than following explicit rules.
- **Deep Learning:** A subset of ML using neural networks with many layers, enabling complex pattern recognition.
- **Large Language Models (LLMs):** A type of deep learning model trained on massive amounts of text, capable of generating coherent and contextually relevant language. Examples: GPT-4, Claude, Gemini.
- **Generative AI:** AI systems that produce new content — text, images, audio, code, video — rather than merely classifying or predicting.
- **Multimodal AI:** Systems that work with multiple types of data simultaneously (e.g., text + images + audio).

> **Key insight:** LLMs do not "understand" language the way humans do. They predict the most statistically likely next token (word fragment) given the input. This makes them powerful and also fallible.

## 2. How Large Language Models Work

Understanding LLMs conceptually helps you use them better.

### Training

LLMs are trained on enormous datasets (books, websites, academic papers, code) containing trillions of words. During training, the model learns statistical relationships between words and concepts by predicting missing text.

### Inference

When you send a message (a **prompt**), the model generates a response one token at a time, using the patterns learned during training. It has no memory of previous conversations unless the conversation history is included in the current prompt.

### Key concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Prompt** | The input you provide to the AI model |
| **Token** | A word fragment processed by the model (roughly 0.75 words on average) |
| **Context window** | The maximum amount of text a model can consider at once |
| **Temperature** | A parameter controlling output randomness (higher = more creative, lower = more predictable) |
| **Hallucination** | When the model generates plausible-sounding but factually incorrect information |
| **Fine-tuning** | Additional training on a specialised dataset to adapt the model to a specific domain |
| **RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** | A technique where the model retrieves relevant external documents before generating a response |
| **Embedding** | A numerical representation of text that captures semantic meaning |

## 3. The AI Landscape in Academia

### AI tools for educators

- **Content generation:** drafting lecture notes, slides, handouts, case studies, exam questions.
- **Student support:** AI chatbots for 24/7 Q&A, feedback on drafts, personalised explanations.
- **Assessment:** automated grading of objective items, formative feedback at scale.
- **Accessibility:** transcription, translation, text-to-speech, language simplification.

### AI tools for researchers

- **Literature review:** AI-powered search and summarisation across thousands of papers.
- **Writing assistance:** drafting, editing, paraphrasing, translation.
- **Data analysis:** code generation, statistical support, pattern recognition.
- **Knowledge synthesis:** connecting ideas across disciplines, identifying research gaps.

### Global adoption trends

- Many leading universities (MIT, Oxford, Stanford) have issued AI guidelines and are actively integrating AI into curricula.
- Major publishers (Elsevier, Springer, Nature) have policies on AI-generated content in research papers.
- UNESCO published guidance on generative AI in education in 2023.
- AI literacy is increasingly considered a core academic competency.

---

## 4. Opportunities and Risks

### Opportunities

- Dramatically reduced time for routine tasks (drafting, summarising, searching).
- Personalised learning at scale.
- Accessible tutoring for students who lack access to human support.
- Accelerated research across disciplines.
- New forms of creative and interdisciplinary work.

### Risks

- **Hallucinations and errors:** AI can fabricate references, statistics, and facts.
- **Bias:** Training data reflects existing societal biases; AI may reinforce them.
- **Over-reliance:** Uncritical use may reduce students' development of foundational skills.
- **Academic integrity:** Blurred lines between AI-assisted and AI-generated work.
- **Data privacy:** Sensitive data entered into AI tools may be used for training.
- **Environmental cost:** Large AI models have significant energy footprints.

## 5. First Steps with an LLM

### Practical exercise

Open any of the following tools (free accounts available):

- [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com)
- [Claude](https://claude.ai)
- [Gemini](https://gemini.google.com)

Try the following prompts:

1. `Explain the concept of [your research topic] to a first-year undergraduate student.`
2. `What are the three most important recent developments in [your field]?`
3. `Generate five exam questions on the topic of [your course topic].`

After each response, ask yourself:
- Is the information accurate? Can you verify it?
- Is the tone appropriate?
- What would you need to change before using this in your teaching or research?

## Key Takeaways

- AI (and especially LLMs) are powerful tools for generating and transforming text, but they are not infallible — they hallucinate, reflect biases, and lack genuine understanding.
- The current AI landscape offers tools relevant to nearly every aspect of teaching and research.
- Effective AI use requires both technical familiarity and critical thinking.
- Your role as an educator or researcher is not threatened by AI — but it is evolving.
