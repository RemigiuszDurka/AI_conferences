## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

- Identify and mitigate the risk of hallucinations and factual errors in AI outputs.
- Recognise common forms of bias in AI systems and their implications for teaching and research.
- Apply institutional and journal policies on AI to your professional practice.
- Navigate academic integrity issues related to AI use in assignments and publications.
- Protect research participant and student data when using AI tools.
- Articulate a personal, principled position on AI use in your professional context.

## 1. Hallucinations and Factual Errors

### What is an AI hallucination?

An AI "hallucination" occurs when a language model generates information that is plausible-sounding but factually incorrect. Common examples include:

- Fabricated paper citations (title, author, journal, and DOI that do not exist).
- Incorrect statistics or dates.
- Inaccurate descriptions of real people, events, or institutions.
- Invented laws, regulations, or institutional policies.
- Confident errors about technical or scientific details.

> **Why does this happen?** LLMs generate text statistically — they predict likely continuations of a prompt, not factually verified statements. They have no built-in fact-checking mechanism.

### Hallucination risk varies by task

| Lower risk | Higher risk |
|-----------|------------|
| Rephrasing or reformatting your own text | Generating lists of references |
| Explaining well-documented general concepts | Providing specific statistics or dates |
| Brainstorming ideas | Describing niche or recent developments |
| Translating between languages | Summarising specific papers you haven't provided |

### Strategies to mitigate hallucinations

1. **Verify everything.** Treat AI output as a first draft requiring fact-checking, not as a source.
2. **Provide context.** Include the actual document (paste the abstract, article, or data) rather than asking AI to recall it.
3. **Ask for sources.** Request citations — then verify them independently.
4. **Use retrieval-augmented tools.** Tools like Perplexity AI and Elicit retrieve real documents before generating text.
5. **Cross-check with authoritative databases.** PubMed, Web of Science, Google Scholar for papers; official websites for facts.
6. **Be especially cautious with numbers.** Statistics, dates, sample sizes, and effect sizes are frequently hallucinated.

## 2. Bias in AI Systems

### Types of bias

**Training data bias:** LLMs are trained primarily on text from the internet, which over-represents English, Western, male, and educated perspectives. This can lead to:

- Systematic underrepresentation of non-Western knowledge and scholarship.
- Gender and racial stereotypes in generated content.
- Assumptions about "normal" or "standard" contexts that do not apply globally.

**Recency bias:** Models have a training cutoff date and may not reflect recent developments in fast-moving fields.

**Representational bias:** AI models may reinforce stereotypical associations (e.g., associating certain professions with particular genders or ethnicities).

**Linguistic bias:** Models perform better in English than in other languages; accuracy degrades for languages with less training data.

### Implications for teaching

- AI-generated content may inadvertently reinforce stereotypes. Review all generated materials for biased language and examples.
- Assessment materials generated by AI may be culturally biased.
- Students from non-Western or non-English backgrounds may find AI tools less effective or relevant.

### Implications for research

- AI literature tools may over-index English-language publications and miss important non-English scholarship.
- AI-generated survey questions may reflect cultural assumptions that do not translate across contexts.
- Quantitative AI analysis may amplify existing biases in datasets.

### Mitigation strategies

- Diversify examples and perspectives explicitly in prompts: `Provide examples from a range of cultural and geographic contexts.`
- Review generated content for stereotypes before use.
- Supplement AI literature search with traditional database searches in multiple languages.
- Consult subject specialists and domain experts rather than relying solely on AI.

## 3. Academic Integrity and AI

### The core challenge

AI tools can generate fluent, coherent academic text in seconds. This creates genuine ambiguity about:

- What counts as "your own work."
- Where the line lies between AI assistance and AI generation.
- How to assess student work in an AI-saturated environment.

### A spectrum of AI use

AI use in academic work exists on a spectrum, not as a binary:

| Clearly acceptable | Grey zone | Clearly problematic |
|-------------------|----------|-------------------|
| Using AI to check grammar and spelling | Using AI to draft sections of a paper | Submitting AI-generated text as entirely your own |
| Using AI to brainstorm ideas | Using AI to respond to reviewer comments | Fabricating data or citations with AI assistance |
| Using AI to search and summarise literature | Using AI to write large portions of an assignment | Using AI to impersonate another person or researcher |

> The boundaries shift depending on institutional policies, course rules, and disciplinary conventions. **When in doubt, declare your AI use and ask.**

### Institutional policies

Most universities now have policies on AI use. Key questions to find answers for at your institution:

- Is AI-assisted writing permitted in coursework? Under what conditions?
- Must students disclose AI use? In what format?
- Does the institution define AI-generated text as plagiarism?
- Are there tool-specific restrictions (e.g., only certain AI tools are approved)?

### Journal and publisher policies

Major publishers have adopted policies requiring disclosure of AI use in manuscripts:

- **Nature, Science, Cell:** AI tools cannot be listed as authors; any AI use must be disclosed in methods.
- **Elsevier:** AI-assisted writing must be declared in a dedicated section.
- **IEEE, ACM:** Similar disclosure requirements; AI cannot be an author.
- **Wiley, Springer:** Disclosure required; policies evolving.

Always check the specific journal's author guidelines before submitting.

### Transparency as a principle

A practical rule of thumb: **disclose AI use at the level of detail you would use for any other tool or assistance.** Just as you would acknowledge statistical software, a research assistant, or a copyeditor, disclose meaningful AI contributions to your work.

## 4. Data Privacy and Confidentiality

### Risks of using AI with sensitive data

When you submit text to a commercial AI service, it may:

- Be stored on the provider's servers.
- Be used (depending on settings and terms of service) to train future AI models.
- Be accessible to provider employees for safety review.

This creates risks when working with:

- **Student data:** grades, learning difficulties, personal circumstances.
- **Research participant data:** interview transcripts, survey responses, medical records.
- **Unpublished research:** novel findings, proprietary methods, confidential datasets.
- **Institutional information:** unreleased policies, personnel matters, financial data.

### Risk mitigation

1. **Anonymise before submitting.** Remove names, identifiers, and sensitive details.
2. **Use enterprise or local versions.** Many AI providers offer enterprise plans with stronger privacy protections. Some tools can be run locally (e.g., Ollama with open-source models).
3. **Read terms of service.** Understand how each tool handles your data. Both OpenAI and Anthropic offer options to opt out of training data use.
4. **Follow institutional guidelines.** Many universities now have approved AI tools with data processing agreements in place.
5. **Apply GDPR principles.** In the EU, processing personal data with AI tools requires a lawful basis and compliance with GDPR.

## 5. Broader Societal Impacts

### Environmental impact

Training large AI models requires enormous computational resources and energy:

- GPT-3's training emitted approximately 552 tonnes of CO₂.
- Running AI queries at scale has a significant ongoing energy cost.

### Labour and economic impacts

- AI is displacing certain types of knowledge work, creating labour market uncertainties.
- The benefits of AI are unevenly distributed across geographies and socioeconomic groups.

### Access and equity

- High-quality AI tools often require paid subscriptions, creating a digital divide.
- AI tools perform worse for non-English speakers and non-Western contexts.

### Misinformation

- Generative AI makes it easier to produce convincing misinformation, deepfakes, and synthetic content.
- This has implications for scientific communication and public trust in research.

## 6. Developing a Personal AI Use Policy

### Discussion activity

In groups or individually, discuss and draft answers to the following questions:

1. **For teaching:** What types of AI use will you permit in your courses? How will you communicate this to students? How will your assessments account for AI?

2. **For research:** What AI assistance will you use in your work? How will you disclose it? What tasks will you never delegate to AI?

3. **For yourself as a learner:** Are there skills you should protect from AI displacement? How will you ensure AI enhances rather than replaces your own thinking?

### Sample policy statement (template)

```
In my courses / research work, I use AI tools to assist with:
[list uses, e.g., brainstorming, editing, literature search].

I do not use AI tools to:
[list prohibited uses, e.g., generate conclusions from my data,
submit AI-generated text without disclosure].

I disclose AI use by:
[describe your disclosure practice, e.g., a note in the methods section,
an acknowledgements statement, a course policy document].

I verify AI outputs by:
[describe verification process, e.g., checking all citations,
cross-referencing with primary sources].
```

## 7. Reflection Questions

1. Have you encountered or suspected AI-generated content in student work? How did you respond?
2. How transparent are you currently about your own use of digital tools (spell-checkers, grammar tools, search engines) in your work? How does AI compare?
3. What are the specific data privacy risks in your own research context?
4. How do you think AI will affect your field and discipline over the next 5–10 years?

---

## Key Takeaways

- AI hallucinations are an inherent characteristic of LLMs — verification is not optional.
- Bias in AI reflects bias in training data; it is the user's responsibility to identify and correct it.
- Academic integrity in an AI era requires transparency, clear policies, and thoughtful assessment design.
- Data privacy obligations apply to AI-assisted work just as to any other data processing.
- Ethical AI use is an ongoing reflective practice, not a one-time decision.
