# References and Further Reading

An annotated bibliography of key works on AI in education and research. All resources are publicly accessible or widely available through academic libraries.

---

## Books

**Mollick, E. (2024). *Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI.* Portfolio/Penguin.**  
An accessible, research-grounded guide to working productively alongside AI. Highly recommended as a companion to this course. Covers both opportunities and risks of LLMs from the perspective of an educator and researcher.

**Russell, S. (2019). *Human Compatible: Artificial Intelligence and the Problem of Control.* Viking.**  
A foundational text on the long-term implications of AI development by one of the leading researchers in the field. Essential background for understanding why AI safety and alignment matter.

**Seldon, A., & Abidoye, O. (2018). *The Fourth Education Revolution: Will Artificial Intelligence Liberate or Infantilise Humanity?* University of Buckingham Press.**  
Explores the transformative potential — and risks — of AI for education systems at all levels.

**Crawford, K. (2021). *Atlas of AI: Power, Politics, and the Planetary Costs of Artificial Intelligence.* Yale University Press.**  
A critical examination of AI's environmental, labour, and political economy dimensions. Essential reading for understanding the broader context of AI adoption.

---

## Academic Articles and Reports

**Bender, E. M., Gebru, T., McMillan-Major, A., & Shmitchell, S. (2021). On the dangers of stochastic parrots: Can language models be too big? *Proceedings of FAccT 2021*, 610–623.**  
Influential paper coining the concept of "stochastic parrots" and raising foundational questions about the risks of large language models. Essential background on hallucinations and training data bias.

**Kasneci, E., et al. (2023). ChatGPT for good? On opportunities and challenges of large language models for education. *Learning and Individual Differences*, 103, 102274.**  
Comprehensive review of opportunities and risks of LLMs specifically in education contexts, covering both student and instructor perspectives.

**Lund, B. D., & Wang, T. (2023). Chatting about ChatGPT: How may AI and GPT impact academia and libraries? *Journal of Academic Librarianship*, 49(2), 102625.**  
Broad overview of GPT's implications for academic work, including library services, student assignments, and research workflows.

**Perkins, M. (2023). Academic integrity considerations of AI large language models in the post-ChatGPT era. *Journal of University Teaching and Learning Practice*, 20(2).**  
Focused analysis of academic integrity challenges raised by LLMs, with practical recommendations for educators.

**Dwivedi, Y. K., et al. (2023). "So what if ChatGPT wrote it?" Multidisciplinary perspectives on opportunities, challenges and implications of generative conversational AI for research, practice and policy. *International Journal of Information Management*, 71, 102642.**  
A broad multidisciplinary examination of ChatGPT's implications across fields including education, healthcare, and policy.

**UNESCO. (2023). *Guidance for Generative AI in Education and Research.* UNESCO.**  
Official UNESCO guidance on responsible use of generative AI in educational and research contexts. Available at: https://unesdoc.unesco.org/ark:/48223/pf0000386693

**European Commission. (2021). *Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI.* European Commission High-Level Expert Group on AI.**  
The foundational EU framework for trustworthy AI, covering transparency, fairness, accountability, and human oversight. Available at: https://ec.europa.eu/futurium/en/ai-alliance-consultation

## Online Courses and Open Resources

**AI for Everyone — Andrew Ng (DeepLearning.AI / Coursera)**  
Non-technical introduction to AI concepts for business and professional contexts. Free to audit. https://www.deeplearning.ai/courses/ai-for-everyone/

**Elements of AI — University of Helsinki**  
Free, widely used introduction to AI concepts. Available in multiple languages. https://www.elementsofai.com/

**Practical AI for Teachers and Students — Wharton Interactive**  
Free course specifically designed for educators, covering LLMs and classroom applications. https://www.coursera.org/learn/practical-ai-for-teachers-and-students

**Ethan Mollick's blog — One Useful Thing**  
Regular updates on AI research and practical applications, written for non-specialists by the author of *Co-Intelligence*. https://www.oneusefulthing.org/

## Staying Current

AI is a fast-moving field. Recommended ways to stay up to date:

- **Newsletters:** The Batch (DeepLearning.AI), The Rundown AI, TLDR AI, MIT Technology Review AI newsletter.
- **Preprint servers:** arXiv (cs.AI, cs.CL sections) for cutting-edge research.
- **Social media/communities:** Follow researchers on LinkedIn or X/Twitter; join professional networks in your discipline that discuss AI.
- **Institutional resources:** Many universities now have AI task forces or working groups — connect with yours.
